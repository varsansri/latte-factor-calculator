The Latte Factor — How Small Daily Spending Destroys Long-Term Wealth

David Bach coined the "latte factor" in his bestselling book to describe a simple but devastating truth: the small, seemingly insignificant purchases we make every day compound into enormous missed wealth over time. Here's the math that will change how you think about every $5 you spend.

The Counterargument — Is the Latte Factor Too Simplistic?

Critics argue the latte factor disproportionately blames individuals for wealth inequality while ignoring structural issues like wages and housing costs. They're partly right — cutting coffee won't solve a systemic income problem. But for people who already have investable discretionary income, the math is undeniably real. The latte factor isn't about guilt — it's about awareness and intention.

The right question isn't "should I cut the coffee?" It's "do I value this daily coffee more than the $182,000 it represents in 30 years?" If yes — drink the coffee, no guilt needed. If no — invest it. The latte factor calculator helps you make that decision with clear numbers, not guesswork.

What Investment Return Should You Use?

  • 7%: S&P 500 historical average (inflation-adjusted). Realistic long-term baseline.
  • 10%: S&P 500 nominal average. More optimistic, less inflation-adjusted.
  • 4–5%: Conservative estimate for bond/mixed portfolios.
  • 1–2%: High-yield savings account. Better than nothing.
